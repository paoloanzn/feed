### Main Topic: Decision Theory

**Main Thesis:** Savage's axioms, which form the basis of Expected Utility theory, inherently allow for situations where an action is strictly better than another, yet the Expected Utility calculation treats them as equally valuable, violating the principle of dominance.

**Simple Summary:** The author proves that the rules used in Expected Utility (EU) theory do not guarantee that rational agents will always prefer a strictly better option over a worse one. Specifically, even when using Savage's foundational axioms, it is possible to define two actions that are strictly better in every possible situation, but the EU framework assigns them the same value. This means the coherence argument—that EU prevents exploitation—is built on a foundation that allows for this kind of blindness.

**The Bottom Line:** > The theoretical framework used to argue that Expected Utility prevents exploitation is flawed because it fails to respect the basic principle of dominance, suggesting that an agent whose preferences are modeled by EU might be vulnerable to being steered into accepting dominated trades.
